Wild Animals of Our World

Click on any animal card to reveal its description and a fun fact.

Carnivores
🦁
Lion
Panthera leo
🔎 Fun Fact
Lions are the only social big cats. They live in groups called prides of up to 30 individuals. They hunt large prey like zebras and wildebeest on African savannahs. Only females do most of the hunting while males guard the territory.
🦈
Great White Shark
Carcharodon carcharias
🔎 Fun Fact
Great white sharks can detect one drop of blood in 100 litres of water. They are apex predators of the ocean and can grow up to 6 metres long. They play a vital role in keeping ocean ecosystems balanced.
🐆
Cheetah
Acinonyx jubatus
🔎 Fun Fact
The cheetah is the fastest land animal on Earth, reaching speeds of up to 112 km/h in short bursts. Unlike other big cats, it cannot roar — it chirps and purrs instead. It is currently classified as vulnerable.
🦅
Bald Eagle
Haliaeetus leucocephalus
🔎 Fun Fact
Bald eagles have eyesight 4 to 8 times stronger than humans. They live near large bodies of open water and feed mainly on fish. They build the largest nests of any bird in North America — sometimes weighing over 1,000 kg.
🐊
Nile Crocodile
Crocodylus niloticus
🔎 Fun Fact
Nile crocodiles are among the most feared predators in Africa. They can hold their breath underwater for up to 2 hours. Despite their fearsome reputation, crocodile mothers carry their babies gently in their mouths to the water.
🐺
Gray Wolf
Canis lupus
🔎 Fun Fact
Gray wolves are keystone species — their presence shapes entire ecosystems. When wolves were reintroduced to Yellowstone National Park in 1995, their hunting of deer caused vegetation to recover along rivers, changing the course of rivers themselves.
Herbivores
🦒
Giraffe
Giraffa camelopardalis
🔎 Fun Fact
Giraffes are the tallest living land animals on Earth. Their long necks allow them to reach leaves high in acacia trees. They only sleep about 30 minutes per day and can go longer without water than camels.
🦏
White Rhinoceros
Ceratotherium simum
🔎 Fun Fact
White rhinos are the second-largest land animals after elephants. They are near-sighted but have an excellent sense of smell. Sadly, rhinos are critically threatened by poaching for their horns, which are illegally sold in some markets.
🦘
Kangaroo
Macropus rufus
🔎 Fun Fact
Kangaroos live in Australia and feed on grass, herbs and shrubs. They carry their young (called joeys) in a pouch on their belly. They can jump up to 9 metres in a single leap and reach speeds of 56 km/h.
🐘
African Elephant
Loxodonta africana
🔎 Fun Fact
African elephants are the largest land animals alive. They eat up to 300 kg of vegetation per day. Elephants are considered ecosystem engineers because they open up forest clearings and dig for water during droughts, helping countless other species survive.
🦬
American Bison
Bison bison
🔎 Fun Fact
Bison once roamed the North American plains in herds of millions. By the late 1800s, they were nearly extinct due to overhunting. Today, conservation efforts have brought their numbers back from under 1,000 to over 500,000.
🐢
Green Sea Turtle
Chelonia mydas
🔎 Fun Fact
Green sea turtles are the only herbivorous sea turtles. They feed on seagrass and algae, helping maintain healthy seagrass beds that support other marine life. They can live for over 80 years and travel thousands of kilometres to nest.
Omnivores
🐻
Brown Bear
Ursus arctos
🔎 Fun Fact
Brown bears eat both plants and animals — berries, roots, fish and small mammals. They hibernate during winter, surviving on fat reserves built up in autumn. A mother bear gives birth while hibernating and her cubs drink milk before she wakes up.
🦝
Raccoon
Procyon lotor
🔎 Fun Fact
Raccoons are highly adaptable omnivores that eat fruits, insects, small animals and even garbage in urban areas. Their "hands" are remarkably dexterous — they can open jars, doors and latches. They are one of the few wild animals thriving in cities.
🐒
Chimpanzee
Pan troglodytes
🔎 Fun Fact
Chimpanzees share about 98.7% of their DNA with humans. They eat fruits, leaves, insects and occasionally hunt smaller monkeys. They are one of the few animals that make and use tools — crafting sticks to extract termites from mounds.
🦜
Macaw
Ara macao
🔎 Fun Fact
Macaws are highly intelligent omnivores that eat seeds, nuts, fruits, and sometimes clay from riverbanks — the clay helps neutralise toxins in their food. They can live for over 50 years. Deforestation threatens many macaw species in the Amazon.
🦔
Hedgehog
Erinaceus europaeus
🔎 Fun Fact
Hedgehogs eat insects, worms, frogs, berries and mushrooms. Their 5,000 to 7,000 spines are made of keratin (the same material as human nails). When threatened, they roll into a tight ball, exposing only their spines to predators.
🐗
Wild Boar
Sus scrofa
🔎 Fun Fact
Wild boars are highly adaptable and eat roots, tubers, insects, eggs, and carrion. Their rooting behaviour turns over soil, helping seeds germinate. They are one of the most widespread large mammals on Earth, found across Europe, Asia and Africa.
Mammals
🦍
Mountain Gorilla
Gorilla beringei beringei
🔎 Fun Fact
Mountain gorillas live in the forests of central Africa and feed on bamboo, leaves and small insects. They live in groups called troops led by a silverback. Their young are called infants. There are only about 1,000 mountain gorillas left in the wild.
🐬
Bottlenose Dolphin
Tursiops truncatus
🔎 Fun Fact
Dolphins are highly social mammals that communicate using a complex system of clicks, whistles and body language. Each dolphin has a unique "signature whistle" — a name that other dolphins use to call it. They are among the most intelligent animals on Earth.
🦦
Sea Otter
Enhydra lutris
🔎 Fun Fact
Sea otters are a keystone species for kelp forest ecosystems. They eat sea urchins, which would otherwise destroy kelp forests. They hold hands while sleeping on water to avoid drifting apart — a behaviour called "rafting."
🦇
Fruit Bat
Pteropus giganteus
🔎 Fun Fact
Fruit bats are vital pollinators and seed dispersers in tropical forests. A single bat can disperse thousands of seeds per night, making them essential for forest regeneration. Without bats, many tropical trees could not reproduce.
🐅
Bengal Tiger
Panthera tigris tigris
🔎 Fun Fact
Bengal tigers are the most numerous tiger subspecies, yet fewer than 2,500 remain in the wild. They are solitary and territorial. Their stripes are like fingerprints — no two tigers have the same pattern. Habitat destruction is the main threat to their survival.
🐨
Koala
Phascolarctos cinereus
🔎 Fun Fact
Koalas are marsupials that feed almost exclusively on eucalyptus leaves, which are toxic to most other animals. They sleep up to 22 hours a day to conserve energy. Australia's 2019–2020 bushfires burned the habitat of an estimated 60,000 koalas.

Wild Animals and Their Habitats

Our Changing World Magazine · Environmental Correspondent

Every creature on Earth depends on its habitat. When that habitat disappears, the entire web of life around it begins to unravel.

Wild animals live in many different habitats around the world. A habitat is the natural home of an animal or plant. Forests, deserts, oceans, grasslands, and mountains are examples of habitats. Each habitat provides food, water, shelter, and space for animals to survive.

In forests, animals like monkeys, birds, and jaguars find trees for protection and fruits for food. In deserts, animals such as camels and snakes survive with very little water. Camels store fat — not water — in their humps, which gives them energy during long journeys across the sand. In oceans, whales, fish, and sea turtles live in salt water and depend on clean water to stay healthy. Every animal adapts to its environment in special ways.

The Role of Each Species

Scientists call some animals "keystone species" because their presence keeps the ecosystem in balance. When wolves hunt deer in forests, for example, deer populations stay under control and plants grow back. When sea otters eat sea urchins, kelp forests survive and provide homes for hundreds of marine species.

"Every living thing is part of a network. Pull one thread out, and the whole fabric begins to fray."

Threats to Natural Habitats

However, many habitats face serious problems. People cut down trees, pollute rivers, and build cities where animals live. These actions destroy natural homes and reduce food sources. As a result, some species become endangered. According to the IUCN Red List, more than 42,000 species currently face the threat of extinction.

Climate change also affects wildlife. Higher temperatures change weather patterns and damage ecosystems. For example, melting ice in cold regions affects animals that depend on icy environments. Polar bears need sea ice to hunt seals. When the ice melts too early, the bears go hungry.

What We Can Do

Protecting habitats is essential. People can recycle, save energy, and reduce plastic use. Schools teach students about conservation, and communities organise clean-up campaigns. When people respect nature, wild animals have a better chance to survive.

When the Forests Fell Silent

A Narrative of Past Environmental Change · The Amazon, 1970–2010

For thousands of years, the Amazon rainforest stood as the lungs of the planet. Then, in the second half of the 20th century, the chainsaws arrived.

In the 1960s, the Brazilian government decided to open the Amazon to development. They built roads deep into the rainforest and offered land to settlers. Thousands of families moved to the region and cleared vast areas of forest to plant crops and raise cattle.

The consequences were immediate and devastating. Jaguars lost their hunting grounds and disappeared from large areas. Tapirs and giant anteaters fled deeper into the remaining forest. Fish populations in rivers declined as trees that once shaded and cooled the water vanished.

Indigenous Communities Paid the Highest Price

Indigenous peoples who had lived in the Amazon for millennia saw their territories invaded. Miners and loggers brought diseases to isolated communities. Some groups disappeared entirely. Their languages, knowledge of medicinal plants, and traditions died with them — a cultural extinction alongside the biological one.

"They burned the trees. They burned the knowledge. They burned the future."
— Environmental activist, Kayapó Nation

A Turning Point

In 1988, activist Chico Mendes was assassinated after years of fighting to protect the forest. His death shocked the world and brought global attention to the Amazon's destruction. International pressure grew. Brazil created protected reserves and conservation agencies. Deforestation rates dropped significantly between 2004 and 2012 — proving that action worked when governments committed to it.

The Amazon did not recover fully. But the forests that remained continued to shelter millions of species. Scientists discovered new species every year. Hope returned — cautiously — to the hearts of conservationists who refused to give up.

While the Ice Was Melting

The Arctic Crisis and Its Wildlife · An Ongoing Emergency

Scientists were monitoring Arctic temperatures when they recorded the most alarming readings in human history. While the world was sleeping, the ice was disappearing.

In the summer of 2019, researchers were conducting their annual survey of polar bear populations on Hudson Bay when they noticed something deeply troubling. The bears were arriving at the coast much earlier than usual — not because spring was coming early, but because the sea ice was breaking up weeks ahead of schedule. Without ice, the bears could not hunt seals. They were starving.

While polar bears were struggling to find food, walruses were facing their own crisis. Traditionally, walruses were resting on sea ice between feeding dives. When that ice disappeared, tens of thousands of walruses were crowding onto narrow beaches in Russia and Alaska — a phenomenon scientists had never seen before.

A Cascade of Consequences

While scientists were documenting these events, other changes were unfolding across the ecosystem. Arctic foxes were competing with red foxes, which were moving north into previously frozen territory. Plankton blooms were shifting earlier in the season, disrupting the timing of fish migrations. The entire food web was reorganising itself in real time.

"While we were debating policies, the animals were already adapting — or failing to."
— Dr. Ian Stirling, polar bear biologist

The Human Dimension

Indigenous communities of the Arctic — Inuit, Yupik, and Inupiaq peoples — were watching their world transform when climate reports were confirming what their elders already knew. The ice was behaving differently. Animals were moving. The knowledge passed down for generations was becoming unreliable for the first time. They were losing not just food sources, but their entire relationship with the land.

The crisis was unfolding at remarkable speed. Scientists were revising their projections every few years, each revision more alarming than the last. While negotiations at international climate conferences were continuing, the Arctic summer ice was shrinking toward its lowest recorded extent.

We Owe This Planet a Debt

Opinion · Wild Futures Editorial Board

I believe that humanity understands, at some deep level, what it is doing to the natural world. The question is not one of knowledge. It is one of will.

I know what the data says. Every serious scientist agrees that we are living through the sixth mass extinction event in Earth's history — and unlike the previous five, this one has a single cause: us. We seem to understand this intellectually, and yet the destruction continues.

I think the problem lies in distance. Most people in wealthy nations do not see a jaguar. They do not hear the silence where a forest once stood. They don't feel the emptiness of a reef bleached white. The suffering seems remote — something that belongs to television documentaries, not daily life.

What We Owe

But I believe that every species that disappears takes something irreplaceable from the world. It is not just a matter of ecology — though the ecological cost is enormous. It is a matter of something harder to measure: wonder. When a child knows there are no more tigers, something changes in how they see the world. The world becomes smaller, less astonishing, more ordinary.

"We do not inherit the Earth from our ancestors — we borrow it from our children."
— Antoine de Saint-Exupéry (attrib.)

Reasons for Hope

I see reasons for cautious hope, however. I notice that young people across the world care deeply about environmental protection. They understand the science. They want change. Governments that once seemed indifferent now feel genuine pressure from their citizens.

I believe — and I want to be honest that this is a belief, not a certainty — that we still have time. The wolves of Yellowstone came back. The bison of the Great Plains recovered. The Amur tiger seems to be stabilising. Nature, it appears, wants to survive. It only needs us to stop standing in the way.

We owe this planet an enormous debt. The question is whether we have the wisdom — and the courage — to pay it.

Final Section

Conclusion

In conclusion, our planet is experiencing rapid environmental changes that affect all living beings. From deforestation to climate change, human actions have transformed ecosystems and endangered countless species. However, there is still hope. By becoming more aware, making responsible decisions, and protecting nature, we can help preserve the balance of life on Earth.
